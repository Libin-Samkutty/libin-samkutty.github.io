Validating Conversational Onboarding for a Global Health Chatbot

Quality Engineering Conversational AI Global Health Regions: India, Kenya, Nigeria Scale: ~2M registered users · 50K+ monthly active Outcome: 80 - 90% improvement in onboarding conversion
ContextAI-driven onboarding redesign for a global health chatbot serving ~200K users across India, Kenya, and Nigeria
ProblemAI classification accuracy and multilingual conversational flows needed validation at scale before production rollout
DecisionLead QA across AI response classification test suites, multilingual flow coverage, and telemetry integrity validation
SystemClassification accuracy QA, onboarding flow validation in multiple languages and regions, telemetry event integrity checks
Result200K+ user onboarding validated across 3 countries, with an 80–90% improvement in onboarding conversion confirmed through telemetry
By Libin Samkutty  

1. The Role of QA When Testing AI-Driven User Experiences

The conversational onboarding project is the most technically complex and personally significant testing engagement I have worked on. It is worth explaining why before getting into the specifics.

The chatbot being tested connected users in India, Kenya, and Nigeria to health information through WhatsApp and Facebook Messenger. Before the redesign, users had to complete a structured demographic form before accessing any content. The form had reasonable intent: collect information to personalize the experience. The result was high dropout. Users who arrived via a WhatsApp link or social media advertisement encountered a form and left before reaching any health information.

The redesign replaced the form with a single conversational prompt: "Tell me a little bit about yourself." The goal was to lower the barrier to entry, gather the same demographic signals through conversation rather than form completion, and route users to relevant content more naturally.

This is a meaningful product improvement with a genuine public health rationale. It is also one of the most difficult things to test that a QA engineer can be asked to validate. The role of QA on this project was not simply to verify that the feature worked correctly - it was to ensure that a system powered by AI, serving users in multiple languages and cultural contexts, with health information at stake, was safe to deploy at scale before two million people were relying on it. That is a different kind of responsibility than passing a regression suite.

2. Key Methodologies and Tools

Why Traditional Testing Methods Are Insufficient for Conversational AI

With a deterministic system, testing is conceptually straightforward. You define the valid inputs, the expected outputs, and the rules that connect them. You write tests that exercise those inputs and assert on those outputs. Edge cases are finite and enumerable.

Conversational AI does not work that way. The input space is unbounded natural language. The system's interpretation of any given input depends on a trained model, not a deterministic rule set. The same words in different contexts may produce different classifications. A phrase that means one thing in British English may carry different implications in Nigerian Pidgin. Slang terms evolve faster than training data. Users communicate in ways that no product team will have fully anticipated.

These properties mean that traditional scripted test coverage, while still necessary, is not sufficient. You cannot enumerate all the ways a real user might respond to "tell me a little bit about yourself." You can only sample meaningfully, define the expected behaviors for the scenarios you have sampled, and build a testing process that gives you reasonable confidence in the system's behavior across the range it will encounter.

Identifying and Scoping the Risks

Before designing the testing strategy, the team conducted a structured risk identification exercise. This produced five categories of risk, each requiring a distinct testing approach:

  • Unstructured inputs. An open conversational prompt generates responses that vary enormously in structure, length, and content. The AI needed to extract useful demographic signals from all of them without routing users incorrectly or producing unhelpful fallbacks.
  • Multilingual and cross-cultural behavior. Users in India, Kenya, and Nigeria would respond in English, Hindi, Swahili, Yoruba, and mixed-language combinations. The system's behavior had to be validated in each of these linguistic contexts, not just in standard English.
  • Silent misclassification. Unlike a bug that produces an error, a misclassified intent routes the user to the wrong content without producing any visible failure signal. These are the failures that matter most for a health product and the failures that are hardest to catch through automated testing alone.
  • Health information accuracy. The content at the end of each routing path was medical and health-related. Misclassification was not merely a UX problem - routing a user seeking maternal health information to a different health topic was a safety concern.
  • Telemetry integrity. The business case for the redesign rested on measurable engagement improvements. If the telemetry measuring onboarding conversion was incorrect, the team would have no reliable basis for assessing whether the change had worked.

3. Technical Insights on Testing Frameworks and Applications

Conversational Flow Testing at the Edge

Standard conversational test scripts cover the happy path and the most obvious edge cases. For an AI-driven onboarding experience, the most important test coverage is in the long tail of inputs that users will actually send. The test suite for this project was deliberately built around ambiguous, incomplete, and off-script inputs alongside the expected ones.

Scenarios included:

  • Minimal responses ("Fine", "OK", "just looking")
  • Overly detailed responses spanning multiple messages
  • Responses that addressed a different topic than expected
  • Responses that included sensitive personal disclosures
  • Responses that expressed confusion or frustration about the prompt itself
  • Partial demographic information that was technically answerable but incomplete

Each scenario had a defined expected behavior. Defining expected behavior for ambiguous inputs required deliberate product decisions that the design team had not always previously documented. The testing process surfaced those gaps - which is one of the underappreciated functions of thorough test case design. The test scripts were written in Botium's conversational format, and input/expected output pairs for ambiguous cases were reviewed with the product team before being finalized.

Building the AI Response Classification Matrix

Evaluating AI-generated responses requires defining what correct looks like for each input category before testing begins. The team built a classification matrix that mapped input patterns to expected routing outcomes with four components:

  • Input categories. Groups of semantically similar user responses that should all produce the same routing outcome, even when phrased differently.
  • Expected routing. For each input category, the specific content path the user should be routed to.
  • Acceptable fallback behaviors. For input categories where the AI should seek clarification rather than route immediately, the expected clarifying follow-up message or behavior.
  • Failure modes to watch for. For input categories where misclassification was especially likely, specific incorrect routing outcomes that testers should document if observed.

This matrix served two purposes: it defined the expected behavior that tests were asserting against, and it created a shared reference that QA, product, and engineering all agreed on before validation began. Disagreements about what the correct behavior should be for an ambiguous input are much better resolved before testing than during it.

Multilingual Validation in Practice

Multilingual testing for a conversational AI product is not the same as testing a localized user interface. Localization testing verifies that translated strings appear correctly and that character encoding is handled properly - it is primarily a visual and formatting concern.

Multilingual AI validation is about whether the model's interpretation of natural language inputs is correct across different languages and cultural registers. This requires testers who are native speakers of the relevant languages and who understand the cultural conventions of how people in those regions actually communicate via WhatsApp.

For this project, testers with native fluency in Hindi, Swahili, and Yoruba were involved in the validation process. Their contribution was not just to send messages in other languages and check that the bot responded in kind - it was to evaluate whether the bot's interpretation made sense given how they would naturally communicate in that language.

One concrete example: in urban Nigerian WhatsApp usage, it is common for users to send very brief initial responses and expand detail in follow-up messages. A model trained on English-language data might interpret a one-word initial response as an insufficient input requiring clarification. A Nigerian tester recognized this pattern as culturally normal behavior that the bot should handle gracefully rather than prompt to expand. This category of feedback is not accessible through automated testing - it requires human testers with genuine cultural context.

Data Collection Integrity Validation

Every onboarding conversation generated demographic data that fed into analytics pipelines, content personalization logic, and program reporting for partner organizations. The testing approach covered four verification areas:

  • Field extraction accuracy. For each demographic signal the system was designed to extract (age range, location, health topic of interest), test cases verified that the correct value was extracted and stored given a range of valid input expressions.
  • Multi-turn state integrity. Onboarding conversations often spanned multiple messages. Tests verified that data extracted in message three was correctly combined with data from message one without corruption or loss.
  • Dropout handling. Users who abandoned the conversation partway through should not have corrupted or inconsistent records. Tests simulated mid-conversation dropout and verified that the resulting database state was consistent.
  • Telemetry event validation. The conversion events used to measure onboarding success were verified to fire correctly under expected conditions and not fire under conditions that should not count as conversions - requiring direct database and event log inspection alongside conversational test coverage.

4. Best Practices for Integrating QA with Development Teams

Involve QA in Defining Expected AI Behavior Before Development Begins

The most expensive place to discover that a team does not agree on what an AI system should do is during testing. When expected behavior is ambiguous and the system is already built, fixing misalignments requires engineering changes, rescheduled releases, and sometimes difficult conversations about scope.

On this project, the classification matrix described above was built collaboratively before the AI system was fully implemented. QA, product, and engineering reviewed it together. Ambiguous cases were resolved in writing rather than in code. By the time test execution began, the expected behavior for every category of input was agreed upon and documented. This requires QA involvement earlier in the development cycle than is traditional - but the argument for this involvement is straightforward: if QA is going to test the behavior, QA needs to understand what the correct behavior is, and the best time to clarify that is before implementation.

Define Separate Acceptance Criteria for AI and Deterministic Components

A conversational AI system has components that are deterministic (routing rules, database writes, telemetry events) and components that are probabilistic (intent classification, response generation). The acceptance criteria for these components should be different.

Deterministic components can be tested with standard pass or fail criteria. Probabilistic components need to be tested against accuracy thresholds - an intent classifier should be expected to correctly classify a defined percentage of test inputs, not to classify every test input correctly. The threshold should be defined based on the risk profile: for health-critical routing decisions, a higher accuracy threshold is warranted than for low-stakes personalization choices.

Run Language and Cultural Validation in Parallel with Technical Testing

Scheduling multilingual and cultural validation as a phase that happens after technical testing is complete introduces risk. If the cultural validation reveals that the AI's behavior is inappropriate for a specific linguistic context, and technical testing is already complete, addressing that finding requires reopening work that was considered done. Running both workstreams in parallel means that findings from cultural validation can inform technical fixes while the technical work is still in progress.

Document and Track AI Failure Modes Over Time

AI systems can degrade. A model that classified intents correctly at launch may produce different results after a training update or after user behavior in production diverges from the training distribution. Quality engineering should maintain a documented list of known failure modes and misclassification patterns observed during testing. This documentation serves two purposes: it defines the scope of manual regression coverage that should be maintained for AI components on each release, and it provides a reference point for investigating whether production issues reflect new failures or known patterns.

The most important outcome was not any individual metric. It was that a system serving two million people in a health context launched with documented, reviewed, and agreed-upon expected behavior, and with known failure modes identified and addressed before any user encountered them. QA on this project was not a verification step - it was a safety function. The distinction matters, and it is the right frame for any quality engineering work where the users are depending on the system for something that genuinely matters to them.
